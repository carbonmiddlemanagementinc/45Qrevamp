Carbon Middle Management -- Policy Analysis
45Q and Contracts for Difference
Historical credit claims, a reformed three-tier structure, and a contracts-for-difference mechanism to close the gap for hard-to-abate industry. Understanding where 45Q has been, where it should go, and what it still cannot reach alone.
This tool models three policy frameworks for Section 45Q carbon capture tax credits: historical credit claims and projections, a reformed concentration-based credit structure, and a contracts-for-difference mechanism adapted from Denmark's CCS Fund for hard-to-abate industry. It is not a CBO score or JCT estimate. All figures are order-of-magnitude and should be treated as illustrative. Credit rates assume post-OBBBA 45Q: $85/t for point-source geologic storage (with EOR now at parity), $180/t for DAC storage. OBBBA (July 4, 2025) preserved 45Q's core structure, transferability, and 2032 construction deadline while adding foreign entity of concern restrictions and creating EOR/utilization credit parity. Historical context
$3.3B over ten years -- pre-IRA rates rarely penciled
From 2015 through 2024, the 45Q credit paid out an estimated $3.3B in total -- nearly all of it through enhanced oil recovery using Class II wells. The pre-BBA rate of $10-20/ton provided marginal additional revenue to operators who would have captured CO2 regardless. The BBA of 2018 raised rates to $35-50/ton and removed the volume cap, but Petra Nova's suspension in 2020 and COVID-era slowdowns kept actual volumes flat. The IRA's jump to $85/ton in August 2022 changed the arithmetic fundamentally for dedicated geologic storage -- but only for facilities whose Class VI well permitting had actually advanced.
The projection step-change
A 10x acceleration driven by infrastructure already in motion
The credit volume ramp from the mid-2020s onward is real -- ExxonMobil's contracted Gulf Coast pipeline, Tallgrass's Wyoming hub, and Air Products' Louisiana blue hydrogen plant represent committed infrastructure, not announcements. The projected range reflects both the concentration of near-term capacity in a small number of large projects and the high project failure rate historically observed by DOE NETL (100 of 149 proposed CCUS projects terminated by 2020). The binding constraint through 2030 is Class VI permitting capacity, not incentive level.
Structural gap
$85/ton is generous for ethanol, insufficient for cement
The flat $85/t credit overpays high-concentration sectors (ethanol capture costs $25-60/t, ammonia $15-35/t) while leaving hard-to-abate sectors stranded (cement $100-160/t, steel $85-155/t). This mismatch is the core policy failure that a reformed, concentration-based credit structure and a CfD mechanism for hard industry would address. The gap problem -- At $85/t, 45Q leaves cement ($100-160/t capture cost), steel ($85-155/t), and refining ($100-165/t) partially or fully stranded. A contract for difference, adapted from Denmark's CCS Fund, tops up the 45Q credit to a guaranteed strike price over a 15-year contract. Bidders submit a fixed CO2 volume per year and a full-chain price per ton. Denmark's 2024-25 tender offered a real-world stress test: 8 of 10 prequalified bidders withdrew, citing compressed timelines, scarce bankable storage, and rigid penalty structures. A US program can learn from these failures by allowing longer development windows, milestone-based (not calendar-based) penalties, and leveraging the Gulf Coast's existing T&S infrastructure to de-risk storage.

Sources, assumptions, and caveats
Historical data
IRS Form 8933 and EIA cross-checks
Historical 45Q credit claims (2015-2024) are derived from IRS Form 8933 aggregate data, cross-referenced against EIA Federal Financial Interventions and Subsidies in Energy Markets reports (2008, 2023). Pre-2018 figures are largely EOR-based claims at $10-20/t. Post-IRA figures include the first dedicated geological storage claims at $85/t. Total historical figure of ~$3.3B is a CMM estimate based on available IRS statistics of income data and should be treated as approximate.
Projections
CMM Low/Mid/High range calibrated against Global CCS Institute facility data
Forward projections are CMM estimates calibrated against Global CCS Institute 2024 facility-level data. Key assumptions: 56% nameplate-to-actual capture ratio (9-facility historical average); 67% project failure/hold rate (DOE NETL; 100 of 149 proposed CCUS projects terminated by 2020); 7-year announcement-to-operation lag. The Low/Mid/High range reflects uncertainty in project completion rates and ramp timing. These are order-of-magnitude estimates using conventional JCT scoring methodology (micro-dynamic, macro-static), not CBO-scoreable budget projections.
Three-tier reform model
30% CO2 concentration threshold with DOE Liftoff cost curves
The reformed 45Q uses a chemically grounded 30% CO2 concentration threshold to separate Tier 1 (post-combustion separation required) from Tier 2 (near-pure byproduct streams). Capture cost ranges from DOE Liftoff Carbon Management report (2023) and Pilorge & Wilcox, EST (2020). JCT estimates use bankable volume x credit x 10yr with a 0.72 ramp discount. Named project deployment thresholds are CMM estimates based on project-specific cost structures and should be treated as illustrative.
CfD model
Danish-model competitive tender, adapted for US conditions
The CfD model is adapted from Denmark's CCS Fund (DKK 28.7B, 2024). Bidders submit a full-chain price per ton covering capture, transport, and storage. The government pays the difference between the winning bid and the $85/t 45Q credit. Denmark's 2024-25 tender surfaced critical lessons: 8 of 10 prequalified bidders withdrew, citing compressed timelines tied to political targets, scarce bankable storage, and strict penalty regimes. A US adaptation should address these by allowing longer development windows (the US model assumes 15-year contracts), using milestone-based rather than calendar-based penalties, and leveraging the Gulf Coast's existing Class II/VI well infrastructure and pipeline networks to reduce storage risk. The tender discount (default 15%) reflects competitive bidding. The model does not account for T&S availability constraints or Class VI permitting timelines.
What is excluded
Tax abatements, state incentives, and T&S bottlenecks
This analysis excludes: state and local tax abatements (which would require project-level county assessor research); state-level incentives such as California LCFS credits or Louisiana industrial tax exemptions; T&S infrastructure constraints (Class VI permitting timelines, pipeline siting); and interaction effects between 45Q and other federal credits. The CfD model also excludes potential revenues from carbon border adjustment mechanisms (CBAM) or voluntary carbon markets, either of which could reduce the required CfD payment in practice.
Uncertainty and precision
Order-of-magnitude estimates, not forecasts
All figures in this analysis should be understood as order-of-magnitude estimates. The JCT-style cost figures carry at least +/-50% uncertainty. Facility counts from the Rhodium/GPI universe are 2021 vintage and may not reflect post-IRA project cancellations or new announcements. Capture cost ranges represent the spread across facilities within each sector, not confidence intervals around a point estimate. The CfD cost estimates are illustrative of mechanism design, not scoreable budget projections.
